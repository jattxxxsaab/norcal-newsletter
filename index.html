📰 Top Stories

What You Need to Know

Interest Rates

Rates Creep Back to 6.45% — But the Long-Term Trend Still Points Lower

After hitting a 3-year low of 6.00% in early March, mortgage rates have climbed back to 6.38–6.45% as of late April. The culprit: rising geopolitical tensions pushed oil prices higher, reignited inflation concerns, and caused the Fed to pump the brakes on rate-cut signaling. Rocket Mortgage's current 30-year rate is 6.625% with a 6.906% APR.

The good news: Morgan Stanley and major forecasters still project rates settling near 5.75% by mid-2026 as inflation cools. Buyers who waited for sub-6% may be waiting longer than expected — but 6.45% is still well below the 7.8% peak in 2023. On a $500K loan, that's roughly $1,400/month in savings vs. peak.

🎯 Takeaway: Don't let the uptick spook your buyers. Rates are choppy but trending down over the year. A 6.45% rate now with a refi window in 6-12 months is a real strategy — get clients into properties while spring inventory lasts.
Market Data

SF Breaks Records — Again — While Inventory Crunch Deepens

San Francisco single-family homes just hit a median of $2.15M in April, an 18% surge year-over-year. Condos aren't far behind at $1.36M, up 27%. The primary driver: a severe inventory shortage — SF has over a third fewer homes for sale compared to March 2025. Yet despite the tighter supply, more listings are starting to trickle in as spring momentum builds.

Well-priced single-family homes in desirable SF neighborhoods are still fielding multiple offers — some topping $1M over ask. The condo market is more nuanced, with record prices masking selectivity; buyers are bidding up only on best-in-class units while mid-tier condos sit longer.

🎯 Takeaway: The SF premium is back and then some. If you have buyers eyeing the city, urgency is real — but quality over speed still wins. Pre-inspections and clean offers continue to be the differentiators.
Investment Spotlight

Central Valley Multifamily: Fundamentals Quietly Improving

The Central Valley multifamily market is entering a favorable stretch heading into late 2026. New deliveries are forecast to fall 35% from 2025 levels, creating a meaningfully thinner supply pipeline. With less competition from new construction, vacancy is expected to ease from current levels down to 4.3% by year-end. Effective rents are stabilizing.

Investment scores back this up: Modesto scores 97/100 on PropertyIQ's February 2026 dataset with a $455K median — the best value ratio in all of California. Fresno scores 82/100 at $415K, the lowest entry price among high-scoring markets. Sacramento secondary submarkets are also seeing strong rental demand as coastal affordability pressure continues to push renters inland.

🎯 Takeaway: The window for Central Valley multifamily entry is opening. Fewer new units competing for tenants + stabilizing rents + below-average entry prices = a setup that doesn't last forever. Modesto and Fresno deserve serious underwriting attention right now.
🚨 Compliance Alert

FinCEN Cash Transaction Reporting — Now in Effect

As of March 1, 2026, escrow and title companies are required to report all-cash real estate transactions involving legal entities or trusts to FinCEN (the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network) as part of California's anti-money laundering compliance push. The rule targets anonymous cash purchases by LLCs, corporations, and trusts — structures commonly used to conceal beneficial ownership.

For most residential transactions with individual buyers, nothing changes. But investment deals structured through entities — which includes many of your investor clients — will trigger mandatory reporting. Escrow and title are handling the filings, but your investors need to know upfront that their transaction will be reported.

🎯 Takeaway: Brief your investor clients before they get surprised at closing. It's not a dealbreaker — it's just paperwork. But no one likes surprises. Add it to your pre-contract investor checklist today.
Regulation Watch

AB 1482 Rent Cap Sunset Is Coming — Multi-Family Investors, Pay Attention

California's AB 1482 statewide rent cap — which limits annual rent increases to 5% + local CPI (max 10%) for covered properties — is set to sunset in 2030, but Sacramento is already debating what replaces it. The broader 2026 legislative session is active with new rental housing bills that could extend, modify, or replace the current cap structure.

For NorCal landlords, the current cap has been manageable in a rising rent environment. But the uncertainty around what comes next is now a real underwriting variable. Multi-family valuations in rent-controlled cities (Sacramento, Oakland) will be sensitive to whatever emerges from this session.

🎯 Takeaway: Don't underwrite Sacramento multi-family deals assuming the current cap forever — and don't assume no cap either. Model both scenarios. Watch the DRE and C.A.R. legislative updates; this will move fast in Q2/Q3 2026.

💡 Agent Pro Tip of the Week

With rates at 6.45% and buyers feeling the sticker shock, start reframing the conversation around monthly payment, not rate. A buyer who locked at 6.00% in March and a buyer locking today at 6.45% are only $130/month apart on a $500K loan — but the psychological gap feels much bigger. Pull up a payment comparison side-by-side in your next buyer consult. Also: remind buyers that today's purchase is tomorrow's refi opportunity. The rate they buy at isn't the rate they're stuck with.
